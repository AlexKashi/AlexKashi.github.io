Current Work

I am currently a researcher at the Stanford Genome Technology Center. We have just submitted or paper our research to PNAS, where we differentiate 20 ME/CFS patients from 20 controls using a novel biosensor approach. The biosensor, named The Nanoneedle, is a highly sensitive impedance based sensor that consists of two 100 nm thick electrodes separated by a middle oxide layer with a thickness of 30 nm. The high sensitivity of the sensor is derived from its small sensing region. In the assay we developed, The Nanoneedle measures a patient's PBMCs (white blood cells), electrical response to an induced osmotic stress over the course of ~2.5 hours. With the goal of developing a classifier for ME/CFS we reduced the dimensionality of the of the impedance data from ~40,000 to 6 using a feature extraction algorithm I designed. We then used PCA on the extracted features to further reduce the dimensionality to 2. In PCA space the patients became separable from the controls using a polynomial kernel SVM.

Past Projects

Flying Courier: Cheap Indoor Drone Localization

[PDF Report]

In this project we proposed an indoor localization scheme for drones only requiring readily accessible materials: an HD webcam an a regular Inkjet printer. It is common practice in computer vision to use two calibrated cameras to obtain the attitude and position of an object. Here we use a single calibrated camera, an object of known size (ARTag), and homography to obtain the attitude and position of an a drone. While a the use of a single ARTag may be acceptable for low speed tracking applications it is demonstrably insufficient for real time safety critical applications. To sufficiently increase the tracking reliability we added a substantial amount of redundancy in the system by designing a large rigid grid of ARTags. Because the size and location of the ARTags are fixed it is possible to use a rigid body transform from each unique ARTag to the origin. If the drone detects multiple ARTags the global position is averaged to remove artifacts.
